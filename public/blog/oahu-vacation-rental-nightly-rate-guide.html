How to Set Your Base Rate

Your base rate is the floor â€” the rate you charge on a typical weeknight in a non-peak month. Everything else (seasonality, events, last-minute adjustments) is layered on top. Getting the base right is essential: set it too high and your calendar fills with gaps; set it too low and you train the market to expect discounts.

Step 1 â€” Identify Your True Comparables

Go to Airbnb and VRBO and search your exact area with your exact specifications: neighborhood, bedroom count, approximate size, key amenities (pool, ocean view, beach access). Filter for listings with at least 20 reviews and a 4.7+ rating. These are your real competitors â€” not every listing in the area, just the ones operating at a similar quality level.

Note their rates during a non-peak period (pick a random Tuesday in October, for example). This gives you the genuine baseline for comparable supply.

Step 2 â€” Identify Your Differentiators

What does your property have that comparable listings don't? Pool? Direct beach access? Enclosed yard? Recently renovated kitchen? Parking for 3 cars? Each differentiator justifies a premium above the comparable baseline. Be honest â€” guest reviews will tell you quickly if you're overestimating your property's appeal.

Step 3 â€” Check Occupancy, Not Just Rate

A competitor charging $700/night with 40% occupancy earns less than a listing at $550/night with 75% occupancy. Dynamic Pricing: What It Is and Why It Matters

Dynamic pricing is the practice of adjusting your nightly rate automatically based on real-time demand signals â€” competitor availability, search volume trends, local events, weather patterns, and booking pace. Airlines and hotels have used it for decades. The vacation rental industry caught up about ten years ago, and the tools available to individual owners are now excellent.

How It Works in Practice

A dynamic pricing tool connects to your Airbnb and/or VRBO listing and adjusts your rates automatically â€” sometimes daily â€” based on the algorithm's assessment of demand. When a local event drives search volume up, the tool raises rates. When a slow period is approaching with open nights, it reduces rates to stimulate bookings rather than let the night go empty.

You set guardrails: a minimum rate you'll never go below, a maximum you're comfortable charging. The tool operates within those bounds. You check in weekly, not daily.

The 5 Pricing Mistakes O'ahu Owners Make

1. Anchoring to What You Want to Earn, Not What the Market Will Bear

Working backward from a revenue goal ("I need $8,000/month, so I'll charge $300/night") is how properties go dark. The market doesn't care what you need â€” it responds to value. Start with what the market pays for comparable properties, then figure out if the economics work at that rate.

2. Ignoring Occupancy in Favor of Maximizing Nightly Rate

A $1,200/night property at 40% occupancy earns $175,200 per year. The same property at $900/night and 65% occupancy earns $213,525. Many self-managing owners optimize for rate as a signal of prestige without tracking the occupancy drag. Revenue is the metric. Occupancy is a variable in the revenue formula.

3. Flat Rates All Year

O'ahu's demand swings dramatically by season, day of week, and local events. A flat rate captures neither the peaks nor converts the slow periods. Owners running flat rates in September and October sit at 40â€“50% occupancy while operators with targeted discounts fill the same calendar at 70%+.

4. Letting Airbnb Smart Pricing Run Unchecked

Airbnb's Smart Pricing algorithm is optimized to get your property booked, not to maximize your revenue. It will frequently recommend rates below market during moderate-demand periods to ensure bookings flow through Airbnb's platform. It is better than no strategy, but it consistently underperforms third-party dynamic pricing tools on annual revenue.

5. Not Adjusting for Last-Minute Gaps

A night that goes empty generates zero revenue. A night that fills at 70% of your normal rate generates real money. Setting rules for last-minute discounts â€” e.g., 20% off any night within 7 days â€” converts what would be empty calendar days into revenue. The psychology of losing the night entirely makes the discount decision easy once you accept it intellectually.
