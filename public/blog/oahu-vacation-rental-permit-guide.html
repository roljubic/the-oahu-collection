The Oahu Vacation Rental Permit Process: A Complete Guide (2025)

February 2026  ¬∑  10 min read

Short-term rental regulation on Oahu is among the strictest in the country. Unlike most mainland markets where you can list on Airbnb first and figure out compliance later, Hawaii's enforcement is real, the fines are significant, and the legal landscape has been tightening steadily since 2019.

If you own property on Oahu and are considering short-term rentals, this guide covers what's legal, what's not, and what the process looks like for properties that qualify.

Disclaimer: This article is for informational purposes only and does not constitute legal advice. Hawaii's STR regulations are subject to change. Consult a Hawaii real estate attorney and verify current regulations with the City & County of Honolulu Department of Planning and Permitting before operating any short-term rental.

The Core Rule: Where Short-Term Rentals Are Legal on Oahu

Under the City & County of Honolulu's land use regulations, short-term rentals (defined as rentals of 30 days or less) are generally prohibited in residential zones unless the property has a valid permit or falls within a designated resort zone.

‚úÖ Generally Legal

  • Resort-zoned properties (Ko Olina, Waikiki, Turtle Bay resort areas)
  • Properties with a valid Bed & Breakfast Home permit
  • Properties with a valid Transient Vacation Unit (TVU) permit
  • Certain hotel-condo projects with STR-permitted unit status

üö´ Generally Prohibited

  • Standard residential zones (R-3.5, R-5, R-7.5, etc.)
  • Properties without an active permit in non-resort areas
  • Properties where the TVU permit waitlist has been closed
  • Apartment zones without a specific permit exception

"The TVU permit waitlist on Oahu has been closed to new applicants since 2019. The only practical path to a legal STR outside a resort zone today is through a Bed & Breakfast Home permit ‚Äî which requires owner-occupancy."

The Two Permit Types: TVU vs. Bed & Breakfast Home

Transient Vacation Unit (TVU) Permit

A TVU permit allows the entire dwelling unit to be rented short-term. The owner does not need to be present. These are the most valuable permits on Oahu ‚Äî and the waitlist is currently closed. Properties with existing TVU permits command a significant premium at sale; the permit often adds $100,000‚Äì$300,000+ to property value.

Key requirements (for existing permit holders):

  • Annual renewal with the Department of Planning and Permitting
  • Payment of annual renewal fee
  • Compliance with transient accommodations tax (TAT) and general excise tax (GET) requirements
  • No violations on record that would trigger permit suspension

Bed & Breakfast Home Permit

A B&B Home permit allows short-term rental of rooms within a dwelling while the owner lives on the property. This is the only path currently open to new applicants in residential zones.

Requirements include:

  • Owner must occupy the property as their primary residence
  • Maximum of two guest rooms (and no more than two guests per room)
  • Off-street parking required for guests
  • No separate cooking facilities in guest rooms
  • Annual renewal and DPP compliance inspection possible
  • Neighbor notification required as part of application process

The Resort Zone Exception: Ko Olina, Waikiki, and Turtle Bay

Properties in designated resort/hotel zones have fundamentally different rules. In these areas, short-term rentals are the intended use ‚Äî and the permitting landscape is considerably simpler.

Notable resort-zone areas on Oahu:

  • Ko Olina Resort ‚Äî four-star resort area on the west shore; many condo-hotel units are STR-permitted by default through the hotel program
  • Waikiki ‚Äî high-density resort zone; most hotel-condo and condotel units can operate as STRs; verify individual unit status with the condo association
  • Turtle Bay Resort ‚Äî North Shore resort; vacation ownership and resort residences with STR programs

Even in resort zones, verify the specific unit's status. Condo association rules can restrict or govern short-term rental even when the city zoning permits it. Some associations require participation in an on-site rental program.

Tax Obligations: GET and TAT

Operating a short-term rental on Oahu triggers state tax obligations that are separate from and in addition to your federal and property tax obligations.

  • General Excise Tax (GET) ‚Äî 4.5% on Oahu (4% state + 0.5% county surcharge), applied to gross rental revenue. Must register with the Hawaii Department of Taxation.
  • Transient Accommodations Tax (TAT) ‚Äî 10.25% on gross rental revenue for stays of 180 days or less. Also collected by the Hawaii Department of Taxation.
  • Platform collection: Airbnb and VRBO collect and remit TAT and GET on behalf of hosts in Hawaii for bookings made through their platforms. If you accept direct bookings, you're responsible for collecting and remitting these taxes yourself.

"The combined GET + TAT rate on Oahu is approximately 14.75% of gross revenue. This must either be included in your nightly rate or itemized as a separate charge ‚Äî guests expect to see it, and failure to remit it to the state is a serious violation."

Enforcement: The Fines Are Real

The City & County of Honolulu actively enforces STR regulations. Complaints from neighbors can trigger inspections. The DPP monitors short-term rental listings on platforms and cross-references against permitted addresses.

Penalties for operating an illegal STR on Oahu:

  • Initial fine: up to $1,000 per day per violation
  • Continued violations: fines escalate; platform listings can be reported and removed
  • Civil liability: repeat violators have faced significant civil actions
  • Listing removal: Airbnb and VRBO will delist properties flagged by local authorities

How to Verify a Property's STR Status Before You Buy

If you're acquiring a property with the intent to operate a short-term rental, these are the verification steps:

  1. Check the zoning designation with the Honolulu City & County DPP ‚Äî honoluludpp.org
  2. Search for active TVU or B&B permits at the property address in the DPP permit database
  3. If it's a condo, review the condo association's house rules for STR restrictions
  4. Confirm the property is in a resort zone (if applicable) via the official zoning map
  5. Have a Hawaii real estate attorney review before close if STR income is material to your acquisition thesis
